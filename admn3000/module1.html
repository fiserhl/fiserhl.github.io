Module 1: Who Works For Whom?

Independent Contractor vs. Employee Classification

The Stakes: Why Classification Matters

Whether a worker is classified as an independent contractor (IC) or employee has profound consequences for everyone involved. This single determination shapes legal rights, financial obligations, and business operations.

Independent Contractor (IC)

  • No payroll taxes withheld
  • No benefits (health, retirement, workers comp)
  • No Title VII employment protections
  • No ADA accommodations required
  • Misclassification: civil penalties + back taxes
  • Company has lower tort liability

Employee

  • Employer withholds payroll taxes
  • Entitled to benefits (statutory minimum)
  • Protected by Title VII, ADEA, ADA, FMLA
  • Workers compensation coverage required
  • Misclassification: wage claims + penalties
  • Company liable for employee negligence (respondeat superior)

The Economic Reality Test

Courts apply a multi-factor test that examines the actual economic relationship between the parties, not the label they use. No single factor is determinative. The IRS and Department of Labor use this framework.

1 Control ▼

Does the company control how the work is done?

An employee is subject to the employer's control over the manner and means of work. An IC exercises independent judgment and controls their own methods. Consider: Does the company set hours? Provide detailed instructions? Require approval before work begins? Dictate the final product format?

Employee Example: Accountant must follow company procedures, use company systems, attend meetings, report to supervisor.

IC Example: Freelance designer chooses their tools, hours, and methods—only the final deliverable is specified.

POINTS TO: EMPLOYEE
2 Integration ▼

Is the work integral to the company's core business?

If the work is directly tied to the company's main purpose, it suggests employment. Peripheral or ancillary services can indicate IC status. For example, a software company's programmers are integral; its janitorial contractor is peripheral.

Employee Example: Software engineers at a tech company perform work central to the business.

IC Example: Outside counsel providing occasional legal advice to the same company.

POINTS TO: EMPLOYEE
3 Permanence ▼

How long and how continuously has the relationship lasted?

Long-term, continuous relationships signal employment. Short-term, project-based work suggests IC status. A worker hired for one specific task differs from one kept on for years. Renewal of contracts and ongoing expectations matter here.

Employee Example: Staff accountant has worked for the company for 5 years with no end date.

IC Example: Consultant hired for a 6-month system redesign project with a defined end date.

POINTS TO: EMPLOYEE
4 Investment ▼

Who provides the tools, equipment, and work location?

If the company provides computers, office space, supplies, and software, the worker is more likely an employee. ICs typically invest in their own tools and infrastructure. This reflects the IC's independent business model.

Employee Example: Company provides office, computer, phone, all software licenses.

IC Example: Freelancer uses their own laptop, software, home office; company pays a flat fee.

POINTS TO: EMPLOYEE
5 Economic Dependence ▼

Does the worker depend on this one company for income?

If a worker relies primarily or entirely on one company for their livelihood, they are economically dependent—a sign of employment. ICs have diverse clients and income streams. A worker who cannot afford to lose this one job is less truly independent.

Employee Example: Accountant gets 100% of income from this company; cannot realistically work elsewhere simultaneously.

IC Example: Consultant serves 8 different clients; any one client represents less than 20% of annual revenue.

POINTS TO: EMPLOYEE
6 Profit or Loss ▼

Can the worker profit or suffer loss independent of the company?

True ICs have the opportunity for profit (smart decisions yield higher income) and risk of loss (poor decisions yield lower income). Employees receive a set wage regardless of company profitability. An IC's compensation varies with business performance and efficiency.

Employee Example: Accountant earns a fixed salary; company profit or loss does not affect their pay.

IC Example: Contractor negotiates a fixed-price project; finishes ahead of schedule and earns a profit, or encounters problems and takes a loss.

POINTS TO: IC

The ABC Test (Alternative Classification Threshold)

Some jurisdictions (notably California under Assembly Bill 5) apply a stricter ABC test. A worker is presumed an employee UNLESS the company can prove ALL THREE of these:

Factor A
Free from Control		 Factor B
Outside Core Business		 Factor C
Independent
Worker is not subject to control or direction by the company regarding work performance. The work is outside the company's normal course of business. Worker is customarily engaged in an independently established occupation of the same nature as the work performed.
Pass: Worker sets own hours, methods, no supervisory oversight. Pass: Company is tech; hires external cleaning service. Pass: Worker has 10 other consulting clients in the field.
Fail: Company mandates hours, schedules, work methods. Fail: Company is ride-share; driver performs core service. Fail: Worker does this work for this company only.
Rideshare Driver (Uber/Lyft): Fails A (app controls routing, acceptance, ratings). Fails B (ride provision is core business). Fails C (not established taxi/consulting business). Result: EMPLOYEE under ABC.
Staff Accountant (Company): Fails A (subject to company control). Fails B (integral to business). Fails C (not independently established). Result: EMPLOYEE under ABC.
