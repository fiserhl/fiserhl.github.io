Module 3: The Burden Shuffle

The McDonnell Douglas Burden-Shifting Framework

Section 1: Title VII Protected Classes

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 protects employees from discrimination based on five core characteristics. Click each class to learn more.

Title VII Core Classes

Race

Title VII, Section 703

Statute: 42 U.S.C. 2000e-2

Coverage: Applies to all races, including white employees and mixed-race individuals.

Examples: Discrimination based on skin tone, racial stereotypes, or ethnic accent.

Color

Title VII, Section 703

Statute: 42 U.S.C. 2000e-2

Coverage: Distinct from race; protects individuals based on shade or tone of skin.

Examples: Light-skinned vs. dark-skinned employees, colorism within the same racial group.

Religion

Title VII, Section 703

Statute: 42 U.S.C. 2000e-2

Coverage: All religions and sincerely held religious beliefs; includes atheism.

Examples: Refusal of accommodations for sabbath, prayer, or religious dress.

Sex

Title VII, Section 703

Statute: 42 U.S.C. 2000e-2

Coverage: Sex, sexual orientation, gender identity, pregnancy (after PDA amendment).

Examples: Gender stereotyping, pregnancy discrimination, unequal pay.

National Origin

Title VII, Section 703

Statute: 42 U.S.C. 2000e-2

Coverage: Country of origin, accent, ethnic association, language.

Examples: English-only policies, accent-based termination, citizenship discrimination.

Additional Protected Classes

Age (ADEA)

Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967

Statute: 29 U.S.C. 621 et seq.

Coverage: Employees 40 years old and older.

Examples: Failure to hire older workers, replacement by younger workers, age-based comments.

Disability (ADA)

Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990

Statute: 42 U.S.C. 12101 et seq.

Coverage: Physical or mental impairments that substantially limit major life activities.

Examples: Refusal of accommodations, disability-based stereotyping, failure to hire due to disability.

Pregnancy (PDA)

Pregnancy Discrimination Act of 1978

Statute: 42 U.S.C. 2000e(k)

Coverage: Pregnancy, childbirth, and related medical conditions.

Examples: Termination upon pregnancy disclosure, denial of promotion due to childbearing plans.

Section 2: The Three-Step Burden Shift

McDonnell Douglas established a framework for discrimination cases. The burden of proof shifts between the employee and employer through three stages. Click each step to learn more.

1 Employee Establishes Prima Facie Case

Who: EMPLOYEE

Burden: Preponderance of the evidence (more likely than not)

What: The employee must establish four elements:

  • Membership in a protected class
  • Qualified to perform the job
  • Suffered an adverse employment action
  • Comparator: a similarly situated non-protected employee treated better

Outcome: If the employee proves all four, the employer's motivation is now suspect. The case proceeds to Step 2.

↓

2 Employer Articulates Legitimate Nondiscriminatory Reason

Who: EMPLOYER

Burden: Production only (articulate a reason, not prove it)

What: The employer states a business reason for the adverse action that is unrelated to the protected class.

Examples of Valid LNRs: documented poor performance, lawful restructuring, legitimate budget cuts, lack of required qualification.

Outcome: If the employer articulates an LNR, the case proceeds to Step 3. This is a low bar—the employer does not yet need to prove the reason.

↓

3 Employee Proves Pretext

Who: EMPLOYEE

Burden: Preponderance of the evidence (clear and convincing)

What: The employee must show that the employer's stated reason is not the true reason—it is a pretext for discrimination.

Evidence of Pretext: Inconsistent application of policy, shift in stated reasons, disparate treatment of comparators, statements by decision-makers about protected class.

Outcome: If the employee proves pretext, discrimination is presumed, and the employer loses.

Key Principle: The burden shifts back and forth. The employee and employer share the burden of proof, but at different steps. The case does not end unless one party fails to meet their burden or the employee proves pretext.

Section 5: Step 3 - Pretext Evidence

At Step 3, the employee must prove the employer's stated reason is false. The strength of pretext evidence varies. Click each card to reveal the evidence and assessment.

Evidence #1: Text Message

Decision-maker's text: "Her energy is vintage. We need fresh perspectives."

STRONG PRETEXT

Direct statement about age (62) creates strong evidence the stated reason is pretextual.

Evidence #2: Hired Younger Worker

Finding: Different department hired younger person for similar role same month.

WEAK ALONE

Stronger with other evidence. Alone, it's circumstantial.

Evidence #3: Comparator Qualifications

Comparator: Selected candidate has significantly lower qualifications (2 years vs. 10 years experience).

STRONG PRETEXT

Clear disparity contradicts stated reason of qualifications. Shows inconsistent standards.

Evidence #4: Company Policy

Finding: Company has a published non-discrimination policy.

NEUTRAL

A policy alone neither supports nor defeats a discrimination claim. It shows intent but not practice.

Evidence #5: Interview Notes

Finding: Decision-maker asked about religion during initial interview, which is prohibited.

STRONG PRETEXT

Illegal inquiry signals discriminatory intent and suggests decision-maker knew discrimination was wrong.

Pretext Patterns: Courts look for evidence that the employer lied or applied rules inconsistently. Strong pretext evidence includes: statements by decision-makers about protected class, selective enforcement of policies, shift in stated reasons, hiring candidates with lower qualifications.
